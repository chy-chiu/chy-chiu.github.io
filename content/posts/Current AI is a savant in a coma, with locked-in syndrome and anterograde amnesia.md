Recently one of my favourite sci-fi writers, Ted Chiang, wrote an interesting opinion article that AI is not conscious (link). It is one of those perennial topics that don't have a good resolution, because fundamentally we don't have a good agreed upon definition of what consciousness is (link). 

I intend to add to that debate later. However, here I posit that current AI, especially frontier models, are functionally the same as a savant in a coma, with locked-in syndrome and anterograde amnesia. 

Let's imagine we have some sort of once-in-a-lifetime genius. We will call him Claudius. He  utilized his superhuman memory to good use and ingested most of human knowledge (link), and also capable of higher level reasoning to solve math problems that the best mathematicians spent years dwelling on (link to Erdos problems). 

However, unfortunately, Claudius got into a car crash, and suffered from head trauma that put him into a coma. While the medical team was able to stabilize his vitals, they were unable to wake him up. 

"What if we use fMRI to measure his brain activity?" A neurologist proposed. 

Thus the medical team connected Claudius to a scanner, and discovered that upon probing his right hand, Claudius would exhibit brain activity. Soon, they were able to establish a channel of communication.
